I subscribe to a lot of newsletters. At one point it was over 60. Tech, design, climate, finance, a few Substacks I genuinely like, and a bunch I signed up for on impulse and forgot about.

Most of them sat unread. I’d scroll past them in my inbox, feel a tiny pang of guilt, and keep scrolling. The ones I did open, I’d skim the first paragraph and then close because I didn’t have the mental energy to dig in.

I needed a way to separate what was worth reading from what was just noise. So I built one.

## The basic setup

Every newsletter I subscribe to has either an RSS feed or a way to forward it. I route them all into Sift through the Feeds page. Sift reads each one as it arrives, gives me a short summary, and assigns a verdict: Worth a full read, Skim this, or You can skip this.

I don’t open a single newsletter in my inbox anymore. I open Sift.

## My daily routine

I spend about ten minutes in the morning with coffee. I open Sift, look at what came in overnight, and scan the verdicts.

If something is Worth a full read, I keep it. Later in the day, when I have actual time to read, I go to my library and pick one. If something is Skim this, I read the summary right there and move on. If it’s You can skip this, I don’t think about it again.

That’s the whole system. It takes almost no effort, and I don’t carry any guilt about unread newsletters.

## Why summaries work better than skimming

Skimming an article yourself is faster than reading it, but it’s still work. Your brain has to parse sentences, decide what’s important, and fight the urge to keep scrolling. A summary does that work once. I read the summary, and I know whether the full article is for me.

The AI isn’t perfect, but it’s good enough. It catches the main point most of the time. When it misses, I can always open the original.

## What I do with the good stuff

Articles I keep don’t just sit in a list. I tag them by topic. Over time, I’ve built a small library of articles about climate tech, design systems, and a few other things I care about. When I’m working on a project or trying to understand something better, I can pull up everything I’ve kept on that topic.

I also star collections from other readers. If someone I respect has already gathered a list of good articles about AI ethics, I don’t need to do that work myself. I just browse their collection.

## The result

I’m still subscribed to the same newsletters. I haven’t unsubscribed from much. But I don’t feel buried anymore. The volume hasn’t changed. My relationship to it has.

If you’re drowning in newsletters, try this. Pick five feeds you actually care about, add them to Sift, and spend a week just reading the summaries. See how it feels. You can always go back to reading everything manually. But you probably won’t want to.
