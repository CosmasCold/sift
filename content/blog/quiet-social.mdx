Most social networks are measured by how much they can get you to say. Comments, likes, retweets, reactions—every platform is a machine for extracting engagement, and every user is a tiny content factory feeding it.

When we started thinking about the social layer inside Sift, we asked a different question. What is the smallest signal that still means something?

The answer we kept coming back to was this: someone kept an article.

That single action says more than a like ever could. It says "I read this and it mattered enough to save." No performance. No hot take. No notification begging for attention. Just a quiet record of what someone found valuable.

If you are new here, I wrote a bit about [why Sift exists](/blog/welcome-to-sift).

## The design constraint

We gave ourselves one rule. No comments. No likes. No follower counts.

If someone follows you, you won't get a notification about it. The count is visible on your profile if you go looking, but it isn't pushed in your face or used to rank you against anyone else.

This rule made everything simpler. It also made everything harder.

Without engagement metrics, we could not rely on the usual dopamine hooks that keep people scrolling. We had to make the experience itself feel good enough that people would return on their own. That meant the design had to be calm, the reading experience had to be fast, and the signal had to be trustworthy.

## What we ended up with

The social layer in Sift is a single feed. You follow someone, and you see the articles they kept. That is the entire product.

There is no algorithm deciding what you see. There is no trending score, no popularity ranking, no "recommended for you" based on engagement. Just a chronological list of what the people you chose to follow decided was worth keeping.

It turns out that when you remove the noise, the signal gets louder. When I see that a friend kept an article about climate policy, I pay attention. Not because it has a lot of likes, but because I know that person and I trust their judgment.

## Why this matters

The internet does not need another place to argue. It needs more places to listen.

Sift's social layer is an experiment in listening. You do not have to say anything. You do not have to perform. You just read, keep what matters, and let that be enough.

If you want to try it, you can follow me on Sift. My profile is public. You will see what I keep, nothing more.
